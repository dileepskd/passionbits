# Personalization at Scale

Personalization drives **50-250% more replies** (Lavender). The key insight: **if your personalization has nothing to do with the problem you solve, it's just an attention hack** (Clay).

## Three Quality Tiers

Not all personalized emails are equal. Aim for Tier 3 whenever possible.

| Tier | Description | What it looks like |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **1. General** | Company-level research only. Basic facts anyone can find on the website. | "Love the way you are empowering Retail and Apparel sectors..." |
| **2. Personalized** | Good research depth, but needs better copywriting. Information-heavy, tends to run long. | Mentions personal achievements and company growth but reads like a research dump. |
| **3. Hyper-personalized** | Deep personal research, short, conversational, authentic. Creates an immediate connection. | Finds a personal similarity, references specific milestones, feels like a peer wrote it. |

**The difference between Tier 2 and Tier 3 is editing.** Keep sentences short, information-heavy, and slightly conversational. Read it aloud. If it sounds like a report, rewrite it.

## Personalization Priority Order

Always research in this order. Move to the next level only when you exhaust the previous one.

### Priority 1: Individual-Level (Highest Impact)

Hyper-personalization works best when you write about something from the prospect's personal life or their oldest achievements. Everyone talks about the new ones.

**Research hierarchy (work through in this order):**

#### 1. Video interviews and Q&A sessions

The highest-value source. Look for video interviews of the prospect on YouTube, LinkedIn, podcasts, or company pages. Interviews contain opinions, personality, and quotable moments.

**How to use it:**
- Watch or skim for interesting points you can tie back to your product or something motivational
- When praising them on their interview, be authentic
- No set format needed. Start with the important information and relate it to yourself
- Then give the pitch and ask if scheduling a quick call/demo is something they are interested in

**Example approach:** "Saw your quick interview and loved your answers. [Company] is obviously a great place to be at and now that you are the bridge, I thought it would be great to convey how we can help you and [Company]'s awesome team."

#### 2. Personal life mentions (articles about them, not by them)

Look for articles, features, or profiles about the prospect. Find interesting quotes, origin stories, or facts that stand out from others.

**How to use it:**
- Look for quotes or facts that tell you about the personality of the prospect
- Focus on their personal journey, not just professional achievements
- Stories about their personal life and how they affected you will resonate with prospects
- Dig deeper. A 2013 video or an old article can be more valuable than recent press

**Example approach:** "I recently came across your Business Leadership video on YouTube. And only one word - INSPIRATIONAL. I am seriously bewildered you grew up on a sheep station and reached this level and working in 30 countries across the world."

#### 3. Personal social media (Twitter, Instagram, Facebook)

Check their personal social accounts for opinions, personal experiences, anecdotes.

**How to use it:**
- Pick up the nuances, not the obvious
- Find something unorthodox and interesting to capture their attention
- Search for something you can truly relate to or something that seems extremely true
- The main takeaway forms your first introduction
- Then explain why you found it useful, inspiring, or how it affected you positively
- Introduce your product and pitch
- Frame the meeting request as a way to connect, not a pitch or demo

#### 4. Blog posts, articles, newsletters they authored

Check their LinkedIn profile for attached blogs, articles, or newsletters. If not there, Google "[company name] + [prospect name]".

**How to use it:**
- Skim the article to find important quotes, thoughts, events, or anything that reveals personality
- Mention the most recent and relevant award or achievement
- Pitch about your product in ONE SENTENCE
- Subject line should reflect the article (e.g., if impressed by their work/comments/views)

**Example approach:** "I was recently going through some articles about start-ups in Australia. That's where I came across your feature on 'Startup Founders'. It's great to see young and humble entrepreneurs like you trying to bring about a change in this world."

#### 5. Supporting articles as conversation starters

Find articles that support or relate to the prospect's opinions and share them as conversation starters. Best done on LinkedIn.

**How to use it:**
- Check their LinkedIn profile for featured blogs, articles, videos
- Google "important stuff" they care about and find a relevant article to share
- Connect on LinkedIn first, then share the article
- After a couple of days of conversation, mention your product in ONE SENTENCE

**Format for connection request:** "Hi [Name], I believe that we share some common interests. I am always looking to connect with like-minded folks on LinkedIn and I would love to add you to my professional network."

**For research papers or in-depth content:**
- Check the summary first for the main takeaway
- Find a related article or paper that discusses a similar topic
- Compare and contrast the opinions
- Use this as a reason to connect and discuss

#### 6. Obvious signals (Forbes 30u30, new role, awards, professional experience)

Once you have exhausted the non-obvious information, go for obvious things. This is easily available information and many people will be using the same to send cold emails.

**How to use it:**
- Spin the information differently so the email does not seem generic
- Instead of just saying congratulations in words, maybe send a digital cake picture or funny GIF
- Don't just state facts. Personalize it a little. Show that you learnt about their growth, saying congratulations makes it seem less transactional
- Find something during research that ties back to your product

#### 7. Manufactured internal conversation (last resort)

When you cannot find any personal information, create a conversation with your colleague/CEO/CTO about the prospect and use that as context.

**How to use it:**
- Think about a specific feature or point that could be brought up in conversation
- Create a natural-sounding conversation via WhatsApp/Slack/Discord
- Take a screenshot and attach it to the email or forward the email
- The forwarded email shows your CEO is interested in the company and includes an offer

**Two formats:**
- **Forwarded email:** CEO asks SDR to reach out to the prospect. Include the forwarded message as context. The prospect sees that a founder/CEO is personally interested.
- **Without screenshot:** Mention "I was having a chat with my CTO the other day and he was super thrilled to know more about your product and its journey."

### Priority 2: Company-Level

If you cannot find anything about the person, move onto the company.

#### 1. Competitor case study reference

Reference their competitor and share a case study of how you helped a similar company.

**How to use it:**
- Research the company and product specifics
- Look for a client working on similar products
- Share the case study to help the prospect see the value you bring to companies like theirs
- Mention mutual connections if available (e.g., a shared connection at the competitor)

#### 2. Portfolio companies and shared investors

Draft an email relating to the company's investors. Works like a "friend of a friend" scenario.

**How to use it:**
- Check Crunchbase for investors. Look under "Financials"
- If backed by the same investor as your existing clients, use the investor connection as a trust signal
- Use the investor connection in the subject line if there is nothing else compelling
- Add 1-2 questions about their finance management and business spends
- Include ONE SENTENCE about how your product could help, focusing on the biggest selling point

#### 3. LinkedIn Sales Navigator growth insights

Use Sales Navigator to gather information about the company.

**Three sections to check:**
- **People:** Identify decision-makers. Reach out to those whose opinions carry the most weight
- **Insights:** See growth in employee distribution and hiring trends. Helps identify which department to focus on
- **Alerts:** See new posts, new hires, and all updates that can help personalize your email

#### 4. Product launch or major company announcement/event

Use a company's announcement on a new product, event, or webinar as leverage.

**How to use it:**
- Check the company's LinkedIn profile for event announcements or project launches
- Read and skim the details about the new product/webinar/event
- Mention your curiosity and anticipation about their product's success
- Shows you have been following the company's work and progress
- Use ONE SENTENCE about your product
- You can also mention what's new at your company (recent funding round, expansion)

**Tip:** The more hyperlinks or call-to-action terms you use, the more likely your email lands in spam.

#### 5. Company LinkedIn posts

Dig deep on the company's LinkedIn. Look at their about section and the general type of content they post.

**How to use it:**
- Scroll through posts and select 1-2 that discuss relevant information
- Notice recurring themes they bring up
- Convey that you were looking up the company without just stating facts (they already know this)
- Then steer the conversation towards your product and pitch

#### 6. Company blog

Check if the company has interesting articles or series you can discuss.

**What to look for:**
- New launches, products, new markets
- Mention the blog and include your thoughts. Use words like "interesting read" to make it personal
- Focus on specifics: not just what they do, but the approach they take
- Show you have taken an interest in what they do

### Priority 3: Persona-Level

Tailor the pitch based on the prospect's role. Different roles have different priorities.

**Note:** Persona-level emails do not have the same depth of personalization. Their strength is their brevity and role-specific relevance.

#### CEO/COO/Director/VP
- Focus is on **growth of the company**
- They think about value in terms of **efficiency and financial benefits**
- Pitch savings, cashback, reduced overhead
- Keep it short. Mention their leadership and achievements briefly

#### CFO/Finance Head
- Main focus is **managing finance activities**
- Your platform reduces their workload
- Pitch expense management, real-time insights, automated reconciliation
- Acknowledge the effort it takes to track cash flow and create financial plans

#### CTO
- In charge of **technical vision**
- Most likely hook: subscription management, virtual cards for SaaS tools
- Pitch vendor management, tech stack expense control
- Reference their technical journey (developer to CTO, awards)

## Email Structure for Personalized Emails

| Section | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| **Introduction** | Hi [Name] |
| **Para 2-3** | Personalization and research. Individual > Company > Industry, in order of priority |
| **Para 4** | Product pitch. ONE SENTENCE. Modify slightly to suit the company's needs |
| **Para 5** | Suggest a meeting/call. Give the client freedom to pick a time |
| **Sign off** | Keep it simple |

## The 3-Minute Personalization System

From "30 Minutes to President's Club":

**Step 1:** Build a research stack of top 10 buying signals: 5 company triggers, 5 person triggers. Stack-rank by relevance.

**Step 2:** Build a 3x3 template: (1) personalization attached to a problem, (2) problem you solve, (3) one-sentence solution + low-friction CTA.

**Step 3:** Create 5 "trigger templates" with pre-written personalization paragraphs for each trigger, with a smooth segue into the problem.

The personalization must logically connect to the problem. A top SDR writes a personalized email in **under 3 minutes**.

## The Four -Graphic Principles (Becc Holland)

- **Demographic** — Age, profession, background
- **Technographic** — Tech stack, tools used
- **Firmographic** — Company size, funding, industry, growth stage
- **Psychographic** — Values, passions, beliefs (highest-impact dimension)

Tapping into what prospects are passionate about drives significantly higher response rates.

## Key Principles

1. **Prioritize non-obvious over obvious.** Everyone talks about the new achievements. Dig for the oldest, most personal ones
2. **Spin information differently.** This is easily available information and many people will be using the same signals. Find a unique angle
3. **Connect research to your product naturally.** The transition from personalization to pitch should feel like a natural conversation
4. **Keep sentences short and information-heavy.** Slightly conversational tone, not a research report
5. **Show genuine interest.** Authenticity gets replies. If you are genuinely interested in the prospect's journey, it shows
6. **One sentence pitch.** Your product pitch should be ONE SENTENCE. Save the details for the call
7. **Give them a reason to respond.** There should be something in it for the client. Frame the meeting as a way to discuss their journey, not just a demo

## Observation-Based Openers (highest performing)

**Trigger-event:** "Congrats on the recent funding round, scaling the team from here is exciting, and I imagine [challenge] is top of mind."

**Observation:** "Your recent post about [topic] resonated, especially the part about [detail]. Got me thinking about how that applies to [challenge]."

**Industry insight:** "Most [role titles] I talk to spend [X hours/week] on [problem], curious if that matches your experience at [Company]."

## What Feels Fake (avoid)

- AI-generated emails with similar phrasing ("I hope this email finds you well")
- Generic attention hacks disconnected from problem ("Cool that you went to UCLA!" then pitch)
- Over-personalizing to creepiness
- "I saw your LinkedIn profile and wanted to reach out" signals mass automation
- Stating facts the prospect already knows without adding your perspective
- Long sentences that dump research without connecting it to anything

## The "So What?" Test

After writing any opening line, read from the prospect's perspective: "So what? Why would I care?" If the answer is nothing, rewrite.
